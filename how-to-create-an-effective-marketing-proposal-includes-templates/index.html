What is a marketing proposal?

A marketing proposal is a formal document presented by an agency or marketer to a potential client, outlining the specifics of a proposed project or service. It encompasses all the essential information needed for the client and stakeholders to make an informed decision about moving forward. Similar to any well-structured business proposal, a marketing proposal follows an initial discovery session where the agency or marketer gathers the necessary data to develop a comprehensive strategy, timeline, and pricing structure. Why you need a marketing proposal

Building upon the fundamental definition and purpose, a marketing proposal is much more than just a presentation of information; it’s a blend of art and science. The scientific aspect lies in the logistics, quantifiable objectives, metrics, timelines, and deliverables. The artistic element lies in the way you present this information, using it to evoke intangible responses from your clients, such as excitement for potential success, trust in your capabilities, and reassurance that their challenges will be addressed. A well-crafted marketing proposal:

  • Furnishes prospective clients with all the necessary details to confidently engage your services.
  • Demonstrates your ability to organize and communicate information persuasively.
  • Provides an opportunity to stand out from competitors while showcasing your attentiveness to their needs and meticulous planning.
  • Emphasizes your expertise and past achievements.
  • Facilitates project outlining and accountability.
  • Safeguards against scope creep and payment issues.

What to include in a marketing proposal

The specific elements of your marketing proposal will vary depending on your services, client, and industry. However, here’s a comprehensive checklist to consider:

  • Cover page: Your initial impression matters! Make it visually appealing, attention-grabbing, and aligned with your client’s brand identity.
  • Executive summary: A concise overview of the primary problem, objective, and proposed solution, providing stakeholders with a clear understanding of the overarching concept.
  • Table of contents: A seemingly minor but crucial element that enables easy navigation through the document.
  • Goals: Clearly define the client’s aspirations. These should encompass both project-specific goals and how they align with broader organizational and marketing goals. Ideally, these goals should be quantifiable.
  • Challenges: Identify the obstacles hindering the achievement of those goals. While your client will likely be aware of most challenges, your discovery session might uncover additional ones.
  • Solutions: Outline the marketing strategies you’ll employ to overcome challenges and achieve objectives.
  • Scope of work: Detail the tactical components of each strategy, including timeframes and operational specifics.
  • Outcome: Define the deliverables for each phase or strategy, demonstrating their connection to the core goals and highlighting any supplementary benefits.
  • Pricing: Present a transparent breakdown of costs for the client, potentially offering various options. Clearly illustrate the return on investment.
  • Working with us: Provide insights into your team composition, company culture, and core values, emphasizing the value proposition for the client.
  • Why us? Showcase testimonials, accolades, performance metrics, unique skills, or social proof that reinforce your suitability for the project.
  • Contract: Outline the terms and conditions and any necessary legal stipulations in a clear and easily understandable manner.

Tips for creating a winning marketing proposal

The effectiveness of your marketing proposal hinges not only on the information presented but also on how you gather, structure, and convey it. Here are some valuable tips to elevate your proposals:

Do your prep work

Treat the proposal as a culmination of a thorough discovery session where you’ve gathered all essential information. Meticulous note-taking ensures that your proposal not only fulfills client needs but also reflects your attentiveness, care, and commitment to their specific preferences. Key information to gather during the discovery session:

  • Short-term and long-term goals and challenges
  • The root cause of the problem
  • Previous/existing strategies, successes, and failures, including experiences with other agencies
  • Current metrics and definition of success
  • Priorities
  • Emotional drivers (frustrations, aspirations, pain points)
  • Personalities and work styles of the client’s team members
  • Timeline, budget, and stakeholders
  • Target audience, brand messaging, and customer journey

Do additional research

While the client is the expert on their business, conducting independent research both before and after the discovery session is crucial. Gathering comprehensive information about the client beforehand demonstrates your diligence, minimizes the need for them to educate you, and allows for a more focused discussion on intricate details. Post-session research on the market, competition, and relevant topics enables you to incorporate statistics and examples that bolster your case and further demonstrate your value.

Make it easy to read and visual

This principle applies to service businesses in their service contracts as well. Avoid complex or overly formal language in an attempt to impress. Maintain a conversational and accessible tone. Use terminology familiar to the client and consistently connect back to their primary concerns and objectives. Visual aids, such as timelines, are invaluable:

Keep it client-focused

While the marketing proposal is an opportunity to highlight your strengths and differentiate yourself, it should always prioritize the client’s perspective. When showcasing your company’s capabilities, consistently tie them back to the value they deliver to the client.

Walk through it with them

Don’t just send the proposal electronically and consider it done. Schedule a meeting to personally guide the client through it. This ensures effective communication of your intended message and allows them to fully appreciate the finer points of your work. After the meeting, provide them with the proposal to review at their leisure, giving them time to absorb the information and raise any questions before proceeding with signing.
