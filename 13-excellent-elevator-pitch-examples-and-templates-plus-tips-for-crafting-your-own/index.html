What is an elevator pitch?

An elevator pitch is a concise promotional message, either spoken or written, designed to convey the value of your product or service and encourage a specific audience to act. Anyone can use elevator pitches, across various fields:

  • Students use them for college applications, internships, and scholarships.
  • Job seekers use them to impress potential employers.
  • Businesses use them to attract clients, investors, and even potential employees.
  • Professionals use them to engage stakeholders.

What makes a good elevator pitch?

A good elevator pitch depends on what you’re pitching, who you’re pitching to, and their familiarity with your offering. However, the most effective pitches are captivating, clear, and convincing because they are:

  • Concise: No more than 30-60 seconds, getting straight to the point.
  • Engaging: Whether using storytelling, humor, data, fear, or another technique, your pitch should grab attention, hold it, and be memorable.
  • Tailored: Like good advertising, an effective pitch is tailored to the audience. Word choice, approach, and highlighted features/benefits are chosen based on their relevance to the listener.
  • Confident: A successful pitch combines confidence (without arrogance) and assertiveness (without aggression).

How to write an elevator pitch

As previously discussed, elevator pitches vary depending on the situation, and you’ll see diverse styles in the templates and examples below. However, here’s a fundamental framework:

  1. Highlight the problem
  2. Introduce your solution
  3. Explain the benefit/value
  4. Highlight your uniqueness

1. Highlight the problem, ideally with a hook

Start with the problem or pain point, as it resonates most with your audience. State it simply, or make it more captivating with a hook. Consider these options:

  • When was the last time you‚Ä¶
  • Picture this:
  • Have you ever noticed‚Ä¶
  • You know how‚Ä¶
  • Is it me or‚Ä¶
  • You know that feeling when‚Ä¶
  • Ever wished you could‚Ä¶
  • Let‚Äôs be real here:
  • Did you know that‚Ä¶. Pro tip Avoid obvious questions like ‚ÄúAre you looking to get more customers?‚Äù or leading questions like “Are you looking for a platform to manage subscription invoicing?” Get creative and authentic. Your aim is to capture attention, not sneak in features and benefits.

2. Introduce the solution

Once the problem is established, present yourself or your business as the solution, the hero. Be specific about how you address the problem, mentioning features rather than benefits. If it’s not clear from your business name, clarify what you offer‚Äîa platform, agency, app, etc. This helps listeners/readers visualize as you speak.

3. State the benefit

Your audience now understands you can solve their problem, but you need to emphasize how it benefits them. Why is eliminating this pain point important? What do they gain? This is your value proposition.

4. Differentiate yourself

The audience knows what you do, how you do it, and its value to them. Now explain why they should choose you over competitors. How can they be sure you’ll deliver? Include your unique selling proposition, social proof, trust signals, or anecdotes.

  • [Solution] is the only [category] that [unique feature].
  • For over [x years], we‚Äôve helped over [X target personas] [achieve benefit].

5. Close with a CTA or question

End with an action item, typically a call to action‚Äîlike buy, register, call, download, sign up‚Äîreinforcing the benefit:

  • Try [solution] today to [achieve benefit].
  • Sign up now so you can experience {feature or benefit} for yourself.
  • Give me a call so we can plan your [ideal outcome].

Alternatively, end with a thought-provoking statement:

  • So let me ask you, how are you going to [solve] your next [problem].
  • So the choice is yours. You can either [old way] or [new way].

Final tips to achieve your best elevator pitch

Here are some final tips for perfecting your pitch:

  • Customize it: Tailor it to your specific audience.
  • Practice it: For spoken pitches, practice until it’s second nature. Write, rewrite, say, recite, record, and listen to it. Practice on someone. Internalize it for a natural and genuine delivery.
  • Keep it clear: Avoid impressing with jargon or complexity. Simplicity aids recall. Speak conversationally.
  • Anticipate objections/questions: You’ll learn common ones over time and can even prepare a secondary pitch to address them. However, avoid being overly defensive in your initial pitch, as it can undermine your confidence.
