9 SEO Copywriting Tips for Achieving Top Rankings (with Examples!)

Mar 01, 2024
5 minute read

It’s widely known that Google constantly adjusts its search algorithm. This continuous improvement benefits users, but it can make SEOs and copywriters anxious. If the rules are always changing, how can you develop a winning strategy?

The good news is: SEO copywriting fundamentals are constant. Adaptability is valuable, but some aspects of SEO copywriting are timeless. This post presents nine enduring SEO copywriting tips for content ranking, with examples from the marketing world. Let’s dive in.

What is SEO copywriting?

SEO copywriting combines SEO (search engine optimization) with copywriting (writing for marketing or advertising). It’s about creating SEO-optimized content. 9 SEO copywriting tips to rank higher

Here’s how to write content that ranks using nine trusted SEO copywriting tips.

1. Grasp (and align with) keyword intent

While most SEO copywriting rules are equally important, keyword intent is fundamental. “Keyword intent” means why people search for a particular keyword. There are three main types:

  1. Informational: Seeking information (e.g., “history of valentine’s day”)
  2. Navigational: Trying to find a website (e.g., “1800 flowers”)
  3. Commercial: Wanting to buy something (e.g., “buy bouquet of flowers”) Google prioritizes delivering the best search results, so aligning with keyword intent is rewarded. For example, targeting “history of valentine’s day” with your flower delivery pricing page won’t rank well because it doesn’t match user intent.

Effective SEO copywriting, whether for homepages, blog posts, or product descriptions, starts with understanding keyword intent. Consider what users seek and provide it.

2. Be direct

Long introductions don’t work well for SEO copywriting. The goal is to create relevant content, which Google prioritizes. While matching keyword intent is key for relevance, demonstrating content pertinence to Google is crucial. An effective method is to use your main keyword early in the copy. 3. Avoid keyword stuffing

There’s a difference between strategically using keywords and keyword stuffing—excessively using keywords to manipulate search rankings. Avoid keyword stuffing because it’s ineffective and provides a poor user experience. Google is too sophisticated for such tactics, and it harms readability. For example: “Email subject lines are important because email subject lines determine whether or not people open your emails. It’s imperative that you A/B test your email subject lines because otherwise you’ll have no idea which email subject lines work best and you’ll be left with middle-of-the-road email subject lines that nobody cares about.” This example is clearly written for algorithms, not humans.

5. Aim for the featured snippet

As Rand Fishkin explained at length on SparkToro’s blog, less than half of Google searches result in clicks. Often, users find answers directly on the results page without clicking any links, primarily due to the featured snippet.

The featured snippet provides concise information, often eliminating the need to click any links. Therefore, when appropriate, aim to secure the featured snippet. If a question can be answered concisely, ensure your content does so. Winning the snippet increases visibility and brand awareness.
